Graduate Researcher

Organization: Amirkabir University of Technology (Tehran Polytechnic), Tehran, Iran
Duration: January 2018 - February 2020
Research:
  • A study of cardiorespiratory related mortality as a result of exposure to black carbon
      • Paper:
      • https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scitotenv.2020.138422
      • This study analyzed black carbon pollution in Tehran and its link to cardiorespiratory deaths between 2018 and 2019. Results showed BC levels, mainly from nighttime heavy-duty vehicle traffic, were strongly correlated with increased mortality, with short lags between exposure and death. Using health impact modeling, the study estimated that reducing BC could prevent over 15,000 annual deaths. The findings underscore the urgent need for emission control measures to protect public health.
        • Farzad et al. (2020)
  • Estimating short-term mortality benefits associated with a reduction in tropospheric ozone
      • Paper:
      • https://doi.org/10.1016/j.atmosenv.2021.118342
      • This study assessed the increasing levels of ground-level ozone in Tehran and its short-term impact on mortality. Analysis showed that ozone concentrations, especially in warm seasons, are significantly linked to increased deaths, particularly from cardiovascular causes, with a 3-day lag after exposure. A 10 ppb increase in ozone corresponded to a ~1% increase in mortality. Modeling predicted that reducing ozone to EPA standards could prevent up to 500 deaths annually. The findings highlight the growing health risks from ozone and the need for measures to reduce its precursors.
        • Farzad et al. (2021)
  • Association between short-term exposure to air pollution and COVID-19 hospital admission/mortality during warm seasons
      • Paper:
      • https://doi.org/10.1007/s10661-021-09210-y
      • This study examined the link between short-term air pollution exposure and COVID-19 hospital admissions and deaths in Tehran during spring and summer 2020. Increases in PM2.5, PM10, ozone, and temperature in summer were associated with higher COVID-19-related hospitalizations and mortality. Ozone showed the strongest and most consistent correlation, suggesting that short-term air pollution exposure may worsen COVID-19 outcomes.
